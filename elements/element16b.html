Essential Element: Part II

Trojan War

How does the Trojan war begin? The story begins, of course, with the famous Helen, said to be the most beautiful of all Greek women. As one might expect of the most beautiful woman in Greece, her parentage is divine; she is the daughter of Zeus and Leda, the wife of the king of Sparta; Helen, therefore is Spartan. She is one of four children and the sister of Agememnon’s wife Clytemnestra. When Helen grows up, she is courted by all the great heroes of Greece, including Odysseus and others, but she chooses the great Menelaus, the brother of the general of the Greek army Agamemnon. Helen lives with Menelaus in Sparta and has a child by him, named Hermione. But at one point when Menelaus is away in Crete, the Trojan prince Paris (also known as Alexander) visits Sparta, seduces Helen and takes her back to Troy. The Greek army is then raised to regain Helen from Troy. Helen’s willingness to go with Paris is presented ambiguously in the Iliad, where she is portrayed both longing for her homeland and attracted to her new lover, and different accounts of the myth in antiquity ascribe greater or lesser culpability to Helen. In some versions even, Helen herself did not actually go to Troy, but only a phantom of her; in such versions of the story, her capture is merely a pretext for war, ordained by Zeus as a necessity in order to reduce the population of the earth.

Essential Element: Part II

Gorgons

According to most versions of the Perseus myth, the three Gorgons lived in North Africa, although some situate them in the far north, in either case at the edges of the Greek and Roman worlds. As indicated above, they are terrifying creatures, with snakes for hair, and whoever looked upon their faces turned to stone. The representation of Medusa and the Gorgons varied in ancient art. Sometimes she was presented in a very human form, but at other times she was more monstrous (see images 1-4). Her monstrosity is connected to the monsters frequently born from Poseidon, for Medusa is said in some accounts to be the lover of Poseidon.

When Perseus arrives at the home of the Gorgons they are all sleeping. Athena helps him by telling him not to look at them. Instead, he approaches them by looking at the reflection in his shield. He beheads Medusa with his scimitar and puts her head in his kibisis, so as to carry it without looking upon he. He escapes from the two other sisters by wearing his cap of invisibility and flying away with his sandals. On his return to Seriphos Perseus encounters the beautiful Andromeda, the daughter of king Cepheus and queen Cassiepea. Cassiepea had boasted that she was more beautiful than the Nereids (sea nymphs) and Poseidon in punishment flooded his kingdom and sent a sea monster to destroy the land. An oracle told Cepheus that the monster would only be appeased if he should offer his daughter Andromeda to it, and he chains her to a rock (a myth which we will see in a future chapter that is very similar to a story told of the great hero Hercules). Perseus kills the beast and marries Andromeda (see images 5-6).She had previously been betrothed to Cepheus’ brother, but Perseus turns him to stone with the Gorgon’s head.
